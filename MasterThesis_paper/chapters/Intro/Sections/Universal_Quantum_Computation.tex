\section{Universal Quantum Computation}

In classical computing, the concept of universality is remarkably straightforward.
A small, finite set of fundamental logic gates—or even a single logical operator like the NAND gate—is perfectly 
sufficient to build and evaluate any arbitrary Boolean function.

Transitioning to the quantum regime, however, introduces a fundamental physical complication: 
the quantum state space is completely continuous.
Because valid quantum operations correspond to arbitrary geometric rotations within a high-dimensional Hilbert space, 
hitting a specific target state is much like trying to manually rotate a dial to a mathematically perfect, irrational angle.
Executing exact, arbitrary unitary transformations would theoretically require a continuously parameterized—and therefore 
infinite—set of logic gates.

To bridge this theoretical gap between continuous unitary operations and the discrete, 
fault-tolerant reality of physical hardware, quantum algorithm design relies heavily on the Solovay-Kitaev theorem \cite{dawson2005}.
This foundational theorem dictates that we do not actually need an infinite variety of gates to navigate the state space.
Instead, any arbitrary quantum operation can be reliably approximated to a desired accuracy, $\epsilon$, using a small, finite, 
and discrete set of basic quantum gates.
Crucially, it mathematically proves that the number of fundamental gates required to achieve this approximation scales only 
polylogarithmically with $1/\epsilon$.
This minimal computational overhead ensures that our need to discretely approximate operations does not accidentally 
destroy the exponential algorithmic speedups inherent to quantum computation.

Consequently, modern fault-tolerant quantum computing architectures standardly utilize the so-called Clifford+$T$ universal gate set.
This standard library typically consists of the Hadamard ($H$), the $\pi/8$ phase gate ($T$), and the two-qubit CNOT gate.
There is a fascinating dichotomy within this set.
Networks composed entirely of Clifford gates (like $H$ and CNOT) are mathematically easy to track and can actually be 
efficiently simulated on a standard classical computer, a strict limitation formalized by the Gottesman-Knill theorem \cite{nielsen2010quantum}.
However, the strategic addition of the non-Clifford $T$ gate breaks this classical simulatability.
Adding the $T$ gate effectively unlocks the ability to navigate the continuous quantum space, 
strictly elevating the discrete set to full quantum universality.
