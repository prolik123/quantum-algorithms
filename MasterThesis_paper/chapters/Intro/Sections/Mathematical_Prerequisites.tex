\section{Mathematical Prerequisites}
To rigorously study quantum algorithms, one must first be fluent in the mathematical language governing quantum mechanics: linear algebra.
Unlike classical computing systems, whose states are typically described by discrete deterministic variables, quantum systems are inherently probabilistic.
Consequently, their foundational behavior is formalized using complex vector spaces, inner products, and linear operator theory \cite{nielsen2010quantum}.

\subsection{Hilbert Spaces and Dirac Notation}
The state of any isolated quantum system is completely described by a unit vector residing in a complex \textit{Hilbert space}, denoted by $\mathcal{H}$.

Intuitively, a Hilbert space can be thought of as a mathematical generalization of standard Euclidean space.
The familiar geometric notions of length and angle still apply, but the space is extended to accommodate complex numbers and theoretically infinite dimensions.
For the finite-dimensional systems relevant to quantum computing—such as a register of $n$ interacting qubits—the Hilbert space is isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$.
This means it is a $2^n$-dimensional complex vector space, an exponential growth factor that naturally hints at the parallel processing power of quantum computers.

To concisely manipulate these state vectors, quantum information science standardly employs Dirac's \textit{bra-ket} notation \cite{dirac1939new}.
This framework provides an elegant algebraic shorthand that maps perfectly to the underlying linear algebra operations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ket} ($\ket{\psi}$): A standard column vector representing a distinct quantum state.
    \item \textbf{Bra} ($\bra{\psi}$): The Hermitian conjugate (conjugate transpose) of the ket, representing a row vector residing in the dual space $\mathcal{H}^*$.
\end{itemize}

For a vector $\psi \in \mathbb{C}^d$, we write:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \ket{\psi} &= \begin{pmatrix} z_1 \\ \vdots \\ z_d \end{pmatrix}, \\
        \bra{\psi} &= \ket{\psi}^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} z_1^* & \dots & z_d^* \end{pmatrix}
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
Here, $z^*$ represents the complex conjugate of the number $z$.

\subsection{Inner and Outer Products}
The \textbf{inner product} of two state vectors $\ket{\phi}$ and $\ket{\psi}$ is a scalar $c \in \mathbb{C}$, denoted by the combined bracket $\braket{\phi}{\psi}$.
Geometrically, this operation measures the "overlap" or mathematical similarity between two quantum states.
It satisfies the following core properties:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Conjugate symmetry: $\braket{\phi}{\psi} = \braket{\psi}{\phi}^*$.
    \item Linearity in the second argument: $\bra{\phi} (a\ket{\psi_1} + b\ket{\psi_2}) = a\braket{\phi}{\psi_1} + b\braket{\phi}{\psi_2}$.
    \item Positive definiteness: $\braket{\psi}{\psi} \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $\ket{\psi} = 0$.
\end{enumerate}

Two vectors are considered mathematically \textit{orthogonal} when their inner product evaluates to zero ($\braket{\phi}{\psi} = 0$). 
This specific condition signifies that they represent entirely mutually exclusive quantum states.
The norm, which corresponds to the geometric length of a state vector, is defined mathematically as $||\psi|| = \sqrt{\braket{\psi}{\psi}}$.
For any vector to represent a physically valid quantum state, it must be properly normalized to unity ($||\psi|| = 1$).
Normalizing the state vector ensures that all possible measurement probabilities will always sum to exactly 100\%.

The \textbf{outer product} of $\ket{\psi}$ and $\ket{\phi}$ forms a linear operator (a matrix), denoted as $A = \ket{\psi}\bra{\phi}$.
Applying this operator to a new vector $\ket{\xi}$ yields a vector proportional to $\ket{\psi}$:
\begin{equation}
    (\ket{\psi}\bra{\phi})\ket{\xi} = \ket{\psi}(\braket{\phi}{\xi}) = c \ket{\psi}
\end{equation}
where $c = \braket{\phi}{\xi}$ is simply a scalar multiplier.
Outer products are heavily utilized in quantum algorithms to construct projection operators, which map directly to physical quantum measurements.

\subsection{Linear Operators}
Quantum states do not exist statically; they constantly evolve.
This evolution is mathematically described by linear operators acting upon the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$.
An operator $A$ is considered linear if it preserves the principle of superposition, meaning $A(c_1\ket{\psi_1} + c_2\ket{\psi_2}) = c_1 A\ket{\psi_1} + c_2 A\ket{\psi_2}$.

\subsubsection{Hermitian Operators and Observables}
An operator $A$ is \textit{Hermitian} (or self-adjoint) if it is perfectly equal to its own conjugate transpose, $A = A^\dagger$.

Hermitian operators are deeply crucial in quantum mechanics because they represent measurable physical quantities, known as \textit{observables} (such as a particle's energy, position, or spin).
The Spectral Theorem states that for any Hermitian operator $M$ on a finite-dimensional space, there inherently exists an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors $\{\ket{m_i}\}$ paired with purely real eigenvalues $m_i$ such that:
\begin{equation}
    M = \sum_i m_i \ket{m_i}\bra{m_i}
\end{equation}
This elegant mathematical decomposition has a profound physical consequence: the outcome of measuring the observable $M$ will always be exactly one of those real values $m_i$.
Real values are strictly required here, as it makes no logical or physical sense to read an imaginary number off a laboratory measurement instrument.

\subsubsection{Unitary Operators and Evolution}
An operator $U$ is \textit{unitary} if its mathematical inverse is exactly equal to its adjoint:
\begin{equation}
    U^\dagger U = U U^\dagger = I
\end{equation}
Unitary operators inherently preserve the inner product: $\bra{\psi} U^\dagger U \ket{\phi} = \braket{\psi}{\phi}$.
Consequently, they fundamentally preserve the norm (length) of any state vector they act upon.

This preservation is absolutely essential for quantum computing.
Because the norm corresponds directly to total probability, unitary evolution acts as a strict mathematical guarantee that the sum of probabilities for all possible outcomes remains exactly $1$ throughout the entire duration of the computation.
Therefore, all logic gates applied to a closed quantum register must be formulated as unitary matrices \cite{nielsen2010quantum}.
