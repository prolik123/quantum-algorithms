\section{Quantum Entanglement}
The tensor product structure naturally gives rise to states that have no classical analog. 
A multi-qubit state is said to be \textit{separable} (or a product state) if it can be written as the tensor product of individual qubit states. 
For instance, the state $\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{2}(\ket{00} + \ket{01} + \ket{10} + \ket{11})$ is separable because 
it can be factored as $\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1})\right) \otimes \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1})\right)$.

However, the vast majority of states in a composite Hilbert space cannot be factored in this manner. 
States that are not separable are called \textit{entangled}. Entanglement represents a profound form of non-local correlation between quantum systems, 
famously described by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (EPR) as "spooky action at a distance" \cite{einstein1935}. 

\subsection{The Bell States}
The most famous examples of maximally entangled two-qubit states are the four Bell states (or EPR pairs). 
They form an orthonormal basis for the two-qubit state space and are defined as:
\begin{align}
    \ket{\Phi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11}) \\
    \ket{\Phi^-} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} - \ket{11}) \\
    \ket{\Psi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01} + \ket{10}) \\
    \ket{\Psi^-} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01} - \ket{10})
\end{align}
Consider the state $\ket{\Phi^+}$. If we measure the first qubit and observe the outcome $0$, 
the state instantaneously collapses to $\ket{00}$. 

Consequently, a subsequent measurement of the second qubit will yield $0$ with 100\% certainty, 
regardless of the physical distance separating the two qubits. 

This perfect correlation violates the Bell inequalities, proving that quantum mechanics cannot be described by local hidden variable theories \cite{bell1964}. 

Because an entangled state cannot be described by assigning definite independent states to its constituent qubits, 
the Bloch sphere representation fails entirely for entangled qubits. The state of the global system is completely known, 
yet the state of the individual subsystems is maximally mixed (completely unknown).
