\section{Quantum Entanglement}

When many qubits are combined, their joint state space is constructe by the tensor product of their spaces.
This structure gives rise to states that have no classical equivalent.
%
%
A state with many qubits is defined as \textit{separable} (or a product state) 
if it can be factored into the tensor product of independent qubit states.
%
%

For instance, the uniform superposition state $\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{2}(\ket{00} + \ket{01} + \ket{10} + \ket{11})$ is separable.
This is because it can be factored into 
%
%
$\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1})\right) \otimes \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1})\right)$. 
%
%
That means changing or measuring one qubit does not force any outcome on the other qubits.
%
%
However, lot of states from composite Hilbert space cannot be factored in this way.
%
%
States that cannot be factored in that way are called \textit{entangled}.
%
%
Entanglement is a form of non local correlation that is used very often in quantum algorithms.
%
%
In a entangled system, the quantum state of the  is whole system may be defined, 
yet the states of the subsystems which it consists of may still be undefined.
%
%
This non-classical behaviour was famously, described by 
Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen (EPR) as "spooky action at a distance" \cite{einstein1935}.
%
%
\subsection{The Bell States}

The most iconic examples of entangled two qubits are the four Bell states, 
also often mentioned in the literature as EPR pairs \cite{nielsen2010quantum}.
%
%
They form a orthonormal basis for the two qubits state space and are defined as follows:
\bigskip
\begin{align}
    \ket{\Phi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11}) \\
    \ket{\Phi^-} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} - \ket{11}) \\
    \ket{\Psi^+} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01} + \ket{10}) \\
    \ket{\Psi^-} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01} - \ket{10})
\end{align}
\bigskip

It is easy to observe that summing the first two equations gives us $\frac{2}{sqrt{2}}(\ket{00})$. In similar ways we can get any of the 
possible states.
%
%

To give some intuitions, let's consider the specific entangled state $\ket{\Phi^+}$.
%
%
Imagine also two experimental parties, named Alice and Bob, each of them having one qubit of this pair.
If Alice will measure her qubit, she will faces a 50\% probability of observing a $\ket{0}$ and a 50\% probability of observing a $\ket{1}$.
%
%
However, after that is the twist. The moment she observes a $\ket{0}$ (the case will be similarly with $\ket{1}$), 
the global wave function of the system collapses instantly from the superposition state into the definite state 
$\ket{00}$.
%
%
If Bob now measures his qubit, he will for sure observe a $\ket{0}$ with 100\% certainty.
%
%
This correlation holds true regardless of whether Bob and Alice are in the same place or far way. The main factor is that in that case, 
this qubits states are strongly connected to each other via system they are part of (which in this case was $\ket{\Phi}^+$).
%
%

This correlation violates the limits established by Bell's inequalities, which is quite important as it proves 
that quantum mechanics cannot be explained or modeled by "local hidden variable" theories \cite{bell1964}.
%
%
In other words, the measurement outcomes are not secretly assigned before they are separated.
%
%

Due to the fact that the entangled system cannot be split into independent states, 
the Bloch sphere for single qubit, fails while trying to visualize the entanglement.
%
%
It is the classical physical paradox here. While we can possess full knowledge about the joint system as a whole, 
we possess no knowledge about its individual parts before the measurement.
