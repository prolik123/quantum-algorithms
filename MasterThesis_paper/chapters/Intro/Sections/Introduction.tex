\section{Introduction}

The theoretical limits of computation have traditionally been bound by the laws of classical physics. 
The Turing Machine, the abstract mathematical model that underpins all modern classical computers, 
operates on a deterministic tape of zeros and ones. 
For decades, the growth of computational power followed Moore's Law, 
doubling the density of transistors on integrated circuits approximately every two years. 
However, as transistor features approach the atomic scale (sub-5nm), 
classical deterministic behavior begins to break down due to quantum mechanical effects such as 
electron tunneling and thermal noise. 

In the early 1980s, physicists Paul Benioff \cite{benioff1980} and Richard Feynman \cite{feynman1982} 
roposed a paradigm shift. Feynman famously observed that simulating quantum mechanical 
systems on classical computers appears to incur an exponential overhead in computational resources. 
He conjectured that a computer itself governed by quantum mechanics could simulate physical 
systems efficiently. This idea was formalized by David Deutsch in 1985, 
who defined the Universal Quantum Turing Machine \cite{deutsch1985}, 
proving that a quantum computer could strictly simulate any physical process.


Unlike classical bits, which exist in a definite state of 0 or 1, 
quantum bits (qubits) leverage two fundamental quantum phenomena: 
\textit{superposition} and \textit{entanglement}. 

These properties allow quantum algorithms to explore a vast Hilbert space of 
computational states simultaneously. 
The discovery of polynomial-time quantum algorithms for problems that are exponentially 
hard for classical computers-most notably Shor's algorithm for integer factorization 
\cite{shor1994}-demonstrated that quantum computing is not merely a physical curiosity, 
but a distinct computational complexity class with the potential to break modern cryptographic 
infrastructure.

This chapter establishes the rigorous mathematical and physical frameworks necessary to 
understand these algorithms. We begin with linear algebra and the postulates of quantum mechanics, 
move to the definition of the qubit and composite systems, 
and conclude with the circuit model of quantum computation.
